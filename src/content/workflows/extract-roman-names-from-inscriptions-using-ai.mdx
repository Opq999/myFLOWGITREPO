## What you'll get
You'll learn how to identify and extract names of individuals from ancient Roman inscriptions using AI. This process helps uncover details about ordinary people, freedmen, and slaves from the Roman era, going beyond just officials. This approach leverages large language models to quickly process historical texts that would otherwise require extensive manual research by classicists.

## Tools you need
*   **Claude** (freemium) — An AI assistant for processing text and extracting information.
*   **Gemini** (freemium) — Another AI assistant for text analysis and information extraction.
*   **Epigraphic Database Clauss-Slaby (EDCS)** (free) — A database of Latin inscriptions to find source texts.
*   **Latin Inscriptions of the Roman Empire (LIRE)** (free) — Another database for Roman inscriptions.

## Steps
1.  **Access an inscription database** — Open a web browser on your phone or computer and navigate to the Epigraphic Database Clauss-Slaby (EDCS) at `https://edcs.hist.uzh.ch/en/` or Latin Inscriptions of the Roman Empire (LIRE) at `https://pure.au.dk/portal/en/publications/latin-inscriptions-of-the-roman-empire-lire/`.

    You should see the homepage of the chosen database, ready for you to search or browse inscriptions.

2.  **Select an inscription** — Browse the database or use its search function to find an inscription. Copy the full Latin text of an inscription you want to analyze.

    You should have the Latin text of an inscription copied to your clipboard.

3.  **Open your AI tool** — Open your web browser and go to `https://claude.ai` or `https://gemini.google.com`. Log in or sign up if prompted.

    You should see the chat interface of Claude or Gemini, ready for your input.

4.  **Prompt the AI to extract names** — Paste the copied inscription text into the AI chat window and use a prompt to ask for names. The author doesn't share their exact prompt; a starting point:

    ```
    I have an ancient Latin inscription. Please extract all proper names of people mentioned in this text, including ordinary people, freedmen, and slaves, if identifiable. List each name clearly.

    [PASTE LATIN INSCRIPTION HERE]
    ```

    You should see the AI process the text and provide a list of names it has identified.

5.  **Review and refine results** — Read through the list of names provided by the AI. If some names seem incorrect or missed, you can ask follow-up questions or provide more context. For example, "Are there any other names in the text that could refer to individuals?" or "Can you explain why you identified [X] as a name?"

    You should have a refined or confirmed list of names from the inscription.

6.  **Optional: Research the names** — Use the extracted names to search other historical databases like Trismegistos (`https://www.trismegistos.org/`) or general search engines to find more information about the individuals or their context.

    You should find additional historical context or related information about the identified names.

## Original source
This workflow is inspired by a project shared by 'metiscus' on Hackernews, where they detailed their journey of building a map of people who lived in the Roman Empire. Unable to find comprehensive lists of ordinary Roman citizens, they leveraged AI tools like Claude and Gemini to process over 500,000 Latin inscriptions from databases like the Epigraphic Database Clauss-Slaby.

## Notes & variations
If you have basic Latin knowledge, even a little, it helps immensely to guide the AI and verify its output. You can also specify the type of names you're looking for (e.g., 'only personal names, not place names') or break down very long inscriptions into smaller chunks for the AI. A common mistake is expecting the AI to be perfectly accurate with ancient languages without any human review; always cross-check the results, especially for critical research.
